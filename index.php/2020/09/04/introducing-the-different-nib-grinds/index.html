Introducing the different nib grinds

2020-09-04 HgXzMnFountain Pen 101

Stub/Cursive Italic:

Stubs are a fairly easy nib grind. They give you fairly thin horizontal stroke and a fairly thick vertical stroke. Cursive Italics are basically sharper and more thin horizontal strokes with thicker vertical strokes. To make a stub grind, simply square the nib by grinding off the underside and tip of the nib. More information here.

Architect/Hebrew’s point:

This is considered to be one of the hardest nib grinds as it involves at least three cuts. It results in the opposite of a stub grind, making a thick horizontal stroke and a thin vertical stroke. To make an Architect grind, first grind the nib into a Zoom nib by smoothing the nib in the position you use it. Then grind the sides of the nib so that it forms a sharp edge.

Zoom:

A zoom nib makes a thicker stroke the smaller your degree holding the pen is relative to the paper. This means that if you hold the pen straight up like a brush, it will be needle-sharp, but if you lower it, it will be buttery smooth. One of the easiest grinds, just smooth the nib to an angle of your liking. The longer you grind, the broader the point is. The degree you grind at will be the lowest degree you can hold the nib at, and also the broadest point.

Oblique:

This nib grind is similar to the stub. It gives you a thin stroke when you move down left and a thick stroke when down right. To make it, make sure to do the exact the same thing as the stub, but rotate the pen to the left when you grind the tip. This makes it comfortable to write with the left oblique, as people naturally rotate their pen to the left when writing. A right oblique is the exact opposite of a left oblique, and the cut is from left to right. Left-handed people sometimes find that it is more comfortable to write with one.

Needlepoint:

The needlepoint is a very unusual grind as it involves resizing a nib. For this grind, make sure to use an extra-fine nib and grind down the sides of the nib. This will make the nib extra sharp and feel like a needle, hence the name. It is almost impossible to make a needlepoint smooth, but for the fine point we do have to sacrifice our comfort. This grind is extremely difficult, as it is hard to control how hard you grind the pen.

Fude:

I don’t have the faintest idea how to do this nib. It literally bends the nib point, making it look ridiculous while having the same affect as a zoom, but a slightly stubbier one.

Concorde:

The opposite of a fude. A fine line when writing it the right side up, and a broad line upside down. I don’t know how to do it, but I do know that after you bend the nib, some extra grinding is needed. This nib is an acquired taste, and I have only seen it in Sailor specialty nibs.
