The Evolution of Writing and Fountain Pens

2020-06-072020-06-07 HgXzMnHistory

The first writing utensils

The first writing utensils were not actually pen and ink or anything close to it: the Ancient Sumerians used cuneiform, which was clay tablets as writing pads, meaning that they didn’t need ink. Instead, the used sticks to imprint onto the clay tablets, then use heat to dry them. The Babylonians also used triangular styluses to carve into the clay. The Ancient Chinese also used writing, but they carved words on turtle shells. Those were cracked under heat to interpret the message of their ancestors. However, such materials to write on had big detractors: they were brittle, easy to break, and expensive. The Ancient Egyptians started to use reed pens on papyrus, which was effective. However, the reed pens easily broke, so they were replaced by quills, that were made from the feathers of large birds. The hollow shaft allowed ink to be sucked in easily. Pencils were made later, out of the rock graphite.

When do fountain pens come in the picture?

To first understand fountain pens, we must first talk about dip pens. Those are pens that have the characteristic nib (point) of the fountain pen, but do not have an ink reservoir. Instead, you have to dip the pen in ink ever so often, true to its name. The first dip pens started becoming popular in 1822 when John Mitchell began mass producing them. Many other companies began to rise—especially in Birmingham—by 1860, there were about 100 companies making steel nibs in Birmingham, though only 12 dominated the trade. There were oblique pen holders that rotated the nib to around 55 degrees that made writing Copperplate and Spencerian much easier.

Fountain pens came into the picture when a system of ink reservoir was patented by many people: one promising one by M. Klein and Henry W. Wynne in 1867 for an ink chamber and delivery system in the handle of the fountain pens. There is, however, some evidence to suggest that Leonardo  Da Vinci is the first one to invent such a pen. During his time, there were only quills, yet he did a sketch showing a pen with a metal nib and ink reservoir. His writing was also likely written with his prototype pen as the ink marks do not fade as if they were dipped again and again but rather a consistent line.

 There were other many fountain pen ink chamber developments and many different filling systems that are developed for the fountain pen that we will discuss here.

Leakage in fountain pens were a big issue: companies like Waterman promptly developed a safety pen which sealed up the fountain pen to prevent such issues. Great companies like Lamy started in Germany, and Pelikan began producing their fountain pens in 1929.

Ballpoints were prototyped in around 1931, but the first affordable fountain pens were made in approximately 1953.
